# Financial Ratios Reference

A comprehensive reference of 25+ ratios organized by category. Each ratio includes its formula, interpretation, typical ranges, and sector-specific notes.

> **Not financial advice.** Educational content only.

---

## Liquidity ratios

Measure a company's ability to meet short-term obligations.

| # | Ratio | Formula | Interpretation | Good | Bad | Sector notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Current ratio** | Current assets / Current liabilities | Can the firm pay bills due within a year? | > 1.5 | < 1.0 | Retail operates lower (high inventory turnover). Utilities run ~1.0 due to regulated cash flows. |
| 2 | **Quick ratio (acid test)** | (Current assets - Inventory) / Current liabilities | Liquidity without relying on selling inventory. | > 1.0 | < 0.5 | Manufacturing/retail naturally lower. Software/services naturally higher. |
| 3 | **Cash ratio** | Cash & equivalents / Current liabilities | Most conservative liquidity test. | > 0.3 | < 0.1 | Tech hoards cash; capital-intensive sectors run lean. |

---

## Leverage ratios

Measure the degree of debt financing and ability to service it.

| # | Ratio | Formula | Interpretation | Good | Bad | Sector notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4 | **Debt-to-Equity (D/E)** | Total debt / Shareholders' equity | How much leverage funds the business? | < 0.5 | > 2.0 | Financials/REITs structurally high (5-10x). Utilities 1.0-1.5 is normal. Tech < 0.3 ideal. |
| 5 | **Interest coverage** | EBIT / Interest expense | Can operating profit cover interest payments? | > 8x | < 3x | Cyclicals need higher coverage to survive downturns. Utilities stable at 3-4x. |
| 6 | **Net debt / EBITDA** | (Total debt - Cash) / EBITDA | Years of earnings to pay off net debt. | < 2.0x | > 4.0x | PE-backed firms often 4-6x. Consumer staples target 2-3x. |
| 7 | **Debt / Total capital** | Total debt / (Total debt + Equity) | Debt as share of total funding. | < 0.35 | > 0.60 | Mirrors D/E but bounded 0-1; easier to compare across sectors. |
| 8 | **Equity multiplier** | Total assets / Shareholders' equity | Amplification of equity through leverage. | < 2.5 | > 5.0 | Banks 10-15x by design. Non-financials > 4x is aggressive. |

## Profitability ratios

Measure how efficiently the firm converts revenue into profit and returns capital.

| # | Ratio | Formula | Interpretation | Good | Bad | Sector notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 9 | **Gross margin** | (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue | Pricing power and cost structure. | > 40% | < 20% | Software 70-85%. Grocery/retail 25-35%. Manufacturing 30-50%. |
| 10 | **Operating margin** | Operating income / Revenue | Efficiency after SG&A and R&D. | > 20% | < 5% | Software 25-40%. Retail 3-8%. Industrials 10-15%. |
| 11 | **Net margin** | Net income / Revenue | Bottom-line profitability. | > 15% | < 3% | Banks 25-30% (leverage). Hardware 5-10%. Pharma 15-25%. |
| 12 | **Return on Equity (ROE)** | Net income / Shareholders' equity | Profit generated per dollar of equity. | > 15% | < 8% | High leverage inflates ROE (see DuPont decomposition). Target >15% with low leverage. |
| 13 | **Return on Assets (ROA)** | Net income / Total assets | Profit per dollar of total assets. | > 8% | < 3% | Asset-light (tech) naturally higher. Asset-heavy (utilities, banks) lower. |
| 14 | **Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)** | NOPAT / (Equity + Net debt) | True economic return above cost of capital. | > 12% | < WACC | The single best measure of business quality. Buffett's preferred lens. |
| 15 | **NOPAT** | Operating income x (1 - tax rate) | Net operating profit after tax; numerator of ROIC. | N/A | N/A | Adjust for non-recurring items. |

## Efficiency ratios

Measure how well the firm uses its assets and manages working capital.

| # | Ratio | Formula | Interpretation | Good | Bad | Sector notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 16 | **Asset turnover** | Revenue / Total assets | Revenue generated per dollar of assets. | > 1.0 | < 0.3 | Retail 2-3x. Utilities 0.3-0.5x. Tech 0.5-1.0x. |
| 17 | **Inventory turnover** | COGS / Average inventory | How fast inventory cycles through. | > 8x | < 4x | Grocery 12-15x. Apparel 4-6x. Industrial 5-8x. Low = obsolescence risk. |
| 18 | **Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)** | (Accounts receivable / Revenue) x 365 | Average days to collect payment. | < 45 | > 90 | B2B naturally higher. Consumer/retail near zero. Government contracts 60-120. |
| 19 | **Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO)** | (Inventory / COGS) x 365 | Average days inventory sits before sale. | < 45 | > 90 | Perishables < 10. Heavy manufacturing 60-90. |
| 20 | **Cash conversion cycle** | DSO + DIO - DPO | Days between paying suppliers and collecting from customers. | < 30 | > 60 | Negative = supplier-financed (Amazon, Dell). |

## Valuation ratios

Measure what the market is paying relative to fundamentals.

| # | Ratio | Formula | Interpretation | Good (value) | Expensive | Sector notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 21 | **Price-to-Earnings (P/E)** | Share price / EPS (TTM) | Years of current earnings priced in. | < 15 | > 30 | Cyclicals: use normalized earnings. Growth stocks: high P/E can be justified by runway. |
| 22 | **Forward P/E** | Share price / Consensus next-12mo EPS | Market expectation of near-term earnings. | < 15 | > 25 | Compare to trailing P/E: forward < trailing = expected growth. |
| 23 | **PEG ratio** | P/E / EPS growth rate (%) | P/E adjusted for growth. | < 1.0 | > 2.0 | Only valid for stable growers. Meaningless for cyclicals or declining earnings. |
| 24 | **Price-to-Book (P/B)** | Share price / Book value per share | Price relative to net asset value. | < 1.5 | > 5.0 | Banks 1.0-1.5x normal. Tech 5-15x (intangible-heavy). Below 1.0 may signal distress or hidden value. |
| 25 | **Price-to-Sales (P/S)** | Market cap / Revenue | Useful when earnings are negative. | < 2.0 | > 8.0 | SaaS 8-15x (high margins). Retail 0.3-0.8x (thin margins). |
| 26 | **Price-to-Free-Cash-Flow (P/FCF)** | Market cap / Free cash flow | What you pay per dollar of distributable cash. | < 15 | > 30 | More reliable than P/E (harder to manipulate). Preferred by value investors. |
| 27 | **EV/EBITDA** | Enterprise value / EBITDA | Capital-structure-neutral valuation. | < 10 | > 20 | M&A standard. Industrials 8-12x. Tech 15-25x. Utilities 8-10x. |
| 28 | **Dividend yield** | Annual dividend / Share price | Income return to shareholders. | > 2.5% | < 0.5% (if paying) | High yield may signal distress. Payout ratio matters more than yield alone. |

## Growth ratios

Measure the rate of expansion in key financial metrics.

| # | Ratio | Formula | Interpretation | Good | Bad | Sector notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 29 | **Revenue CAGR (5yr)** | (Rev_now / Rev_5yr_ago)^(1/5) - 1 | Annualized top-line growth. | > 8% | < 0% | Mature companies 3-5%. High-growth 20%+. Decliners: thesis risk. |
| 30 | **EPS CAGR (5yr)** | (EPS_now / EPS_5yr_ago)^(1/5) - 1 | Earnings growth rate. | > 10% | < 0% | Can diverge from revenue CAGR via margin expansion or buybacks. |
| 31 | **FCF CAGR (5yr)** | (FCF_now / FCF_5yr_ago)^(1/5) - 1 | Cash generation growth rate. | > 8% | < 0% | Most honest growth metric -- harder to manipulate than EPS. |

## How to use this table

1. **Screen.** Use thresholds in the "Good" column to filter a universe down to candidates (see `watchlist-and-screening`).
2. **Diagnose.** Once a name passes screening, compute all relevant ratios for the last 10 years and look for trends (stable/improving = good, deteriorating = bad).
3. **Compare.** Ratios are most meaningful relative to: (a) the company's own history, (b) direct competitors, (c) the sector median.
4. **Do not optimize a single ratio.** A high ROE with high leverage is not the same as a high ROE with low leverage. Use DuPont decomposition (ROE = margin x turnover x leverage) to understand what drives the number.
5. **Normalize for cycles.** Cyclical businesses look best at the peak (high margins, low P/E) and worst at the trough. Use mid-cycle averages.

## DuPont decomposition of ROE

```
ROE = Net margin x Asset turnover x Equity multiplier
    = (Net income / Revenue) x (Revenue / Assets) x (Assets / Equity)
```

This decomposition reveals whether high ROE comes from:
- High margins (good -- pricing power)
- High asset efficiency (good -- capital-light)
- High leverage (dangerous -- amplifies both gains and losses)

Prefer ROE driven by margins and turnover, not leverage.

## Ratio red flags (automatic Z triggers)

- ROIC < WACC for 3+ consecutive years: value destruction.
- Interest coverage < 2x: distress risk.
- Cash conversion cycle increasing year-over-year for 3+ years: working capital deterioration.
- Revenue growing but FCF declining: earnings quality problem.
- Gross margin declining > 3% over 5 years: moat erosion signal.
- P/E > 30 with PEG > 2: overvaluation with no growth justification.
- Net debt / EBITDA > 4x outside of regulated utilities/REITs: overleveraged.
